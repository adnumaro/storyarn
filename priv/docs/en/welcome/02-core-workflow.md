Every team uses Storyarn differently, but here's how a typical project flows from setup to localization.

---

## Set up your space

Create a **workspace** for your team, then create a **project** inside it. Each project is self-contained — its own sheets, flows, scenes, and screenplays.

Invite team members and assign roles. Workspace roles control who can manage the team. Project roles control who can edit content.

---

## Define your world

Start with **Sheets**. Create character profiles, item databases, location details — anything you need to track as structured data.

Each field on a sheet becomes a **variable**. A character's health, a quest's status, an item's damage — all referenceable from your flows.

---

## Build your narrative

Create **Flows** for dialogue and quest logic. Connect dialogue nodes, add conditions that check your variables, and add instructions that modify them.

Test everything with the built-in **debugger** — step through nodes, watch variables change, and verify every branch works before it reaches your game engine.

---

## Map your world

Lay out locations in **Scenes**. Draw zones for areas, add pins for points of interest, and connect them to define how players navigate your world.

Link zones to flows so clicking a location opens the relevant dialogue.

---

## Write and export scripts

Format your narrative in **Screenplays**. Write directly in screenplay format or sync from flows. Export to **Fountain** for Final Draft, Highland, or any compatible tool.

---

## Localize everything

When content is ready, the **Localization** tools extract all translatable texts. Use DeepL for a first pass, maintain a glossary for consistency, then export for professional translators.

Track progress with reports that show what's translated, what's missing, and what's gone stale.

---

## Collaborate in real time

Throughout all of this, your team works together. See who's online, watch live cursors on canvases, and let automatic locking prevent conflicting edits.
